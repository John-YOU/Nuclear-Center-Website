Nanostructured Materials for Sustainable Development

Sustainable development is of essence for the Energetic materials are substances that store chemical energy. Conventional energetic materials can be classified into different classes, i.e. propellants, explosives and pyrotechnics. Propellants and pyrotechnics release their energy through relatively slow deflagration processes that last for several seconds (combustion). Explosives release their energy in fast detonation processes (microsecond timescale). Traditional energetic materials (monomolecular or composite) have drawn a growing interest in the scientific community and civil industry since they are very attractive sources of onboard energy to generate gas, heat, and power. Typically, the combustion of propellant produces 5 MJ/kg, while a modern chemical lithium battery used in new laptops only stores 0.5 MJ/kg. Therefore, energetic materials could have a major impact on microenergetic field.

New energetic materials are the key to great advances in microscale energy-demanding systems as actuation part, igniter, propulsion unit, and power. Nanotechnology has stimulated revolutionary advances in many scientific and industrial fields, particularly in energetic materials. Nanostructured energetic materials can be prepared through a delicately designed process using various nanotechnologies. Consequently, nanostructured energetic materials particularly offer the promise of much higher energy densities, faster rate of energy release, greater stability, and more security (sensitivity to unwanted initiation). We will design, synthesize and characterize novel nanostructured energetic materials, and explore their promising applications in micro power generation, hydrogen production, and energy storage and transfer for sustainable development.

Widely used portable devices require portable power sources and various portable devices demand that the power is accurately controlled. Research for non-conventional energy sources is receiving intensive attention due to the disappearing natural resources and increasing concern for environmental safety. As aforementioned, energetic materials have very high energy density, which could be utilized to release energy and generate power. We will develop methods to generate power by combustion of nanostructured energetic materials for portable devices and small/microscale devices applications. The nanostructured energetic materials will be synthesized onto substrates. Igniters are then placed on the nanostructured energetic materials. When power is needed, the nanostructured energetic materials are ignited. Then, various forms of energy, mainly including thermal, mechanical, magnetic, and optic energy, can be exploited and converted to electrical energy via corresponding thermoelectric, piezoelectric, and magneto transducers.

Air pollution is a serious public health problem throughout the world. In industrialized and developing countries, motor vehicle emissions are major contributors to urban air quality. Hydrogen is one of the clean fuel options for reducing motor vehicle emissions. Hydrogen has a strategic importance in the pursuit of a low-emission, environment-benign, cleaner and more sustainable energy system. Combustion product of hydrogen is clean, which consists of water and a little amount of nitrogen oxides. Hydrogen has very special properties as a transportation fuel, including a rapid burning speed, a high effective octane number, and no toxicity or ozone-forming potential. We will develop aluminum-based nanostructured energetic materials for hydrogen production in the aluminum-water split reaction. We will synthesize nanostructured energetic materials by combining aluminum with a set of metal oxides including those capable of oxidizing aluminum in thermite reactions. Experiments used a water displacement method will be performed to quantify hydrogen production so as to explore the ability of these nanostructured energetic materials to achieve continuous and controlled aluminum-water reaction for generating hydrogen. The reaction mechanism will also be studied to assign different rate controlling processes to different stages of the aluminum-water split reaction for the nanostructured energetic materials.

Nanostructured energetic materials exhibit enhanced thermal properties (including thermal capacity) compared with their micron-sized counterparts, which enable them to uniformly distribute the absorbed energy and maintain this energy for an extended duration. Therefore, sufficient energy can be applied first (just below the activation energy threshold) to the nanostructured energetic materials and they can store the energy for an extended period. The nanostructured energetic materials can produce this extra energy apart from intrinsic chemical energy once the reaction is triggered. We will synthesize aluminum and magnesium based nanostructured energetic materials and explore their applications in energy storage and transfer. Ignition of the nanostructured energetic materials leads to a slow-controlled, self-propagating, high temperature conversion of chemical energy to thermal energy. This thermal energy can then be channeled to produce steam that can be used for power generation. They could also potentially replace gaseous fuels such as propane and supply heat energy without hydrocarbon by-products.

In addition to the aforementioned nanostructured energetic materials, we will also explore nanostructured metal-oxides for energy storage applications. With ever-increasing concerns over environmental pollution and depletion of fossil fuels, high-performance and safe alternative energy storage devices have sparked a world-wide research interest for sustainable development. As two major devices for electrochemical energy storage, lithium-ion batteries and supercapacitors have attracted considerable attention as they show great promise as power sources for diverse applications, ranging from portable electronics to electric vehicles or hybrid electric vehicles, owing to their superior electrochemical performance in association with safety characteristics and ecological features. The performance of these devices depends intimately on the properties of their electrode materials. We will design, synthesize and characterize novel three-dimensional hierarchical nanostructures based on metal-oxides and constructed from interconnected nano-building blocks directly grown on conductive current collectors as promising electrode materials for lithium-ion batteries and supercapacitors. These nanostructured electrode materials have low-cost, high specific capacitance, and outstanding rate capability. Especially, they can maintain high performance in severe conditions; for instance they have excellent cycling stability to bear the large volume expansion and contraction during long-term charge and discharge cycling processes.
